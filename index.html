Serendipity Interface: A Rerouting of Downtown Manhattan
Benny Yang
Research questions
How might we prioritize serendipity, surprise, and subjectivity over speed and efficiency in urban navigation systems?
What happens when we design maps to reflect mood and desire rather than utility?
Can computational tools be playful, emotional, and ambiguous — not just rational?
How might this reflect, critique, or offer alternatives to normative technologies of urban navigation?

Historical Lineage
The Situationist International: Guy Debord’s dérive as a method of experiencing space through emotion and chance; détournement as subversion of dominant spatial logic.

Psychogeographic Cartography: Debord’s Guide Psychogéographique de Paris, counter-maps made by artists and activists.

Design Fiction & Speculative Interfaces: Dunne & Raby’s Speculative Everything, creating technology not to solve problems, but to ask better questions.

Sketchpad marked the birth of computational drawing and the graphic user interface, embedding ideas of precision, parametric logic, and user-agency into design software. It introduced the computer as a medium for modeling — for encoding geometry, constraints, and control. It laid the groundwork for CAD, and by extension, the entire logic of optimization that underpins modern computational design. My project emerges from this lineage but turns its assumptions sideways. Where Sketchpad prioritized constraint-solving, clarity, and structure, the Serendipity Map seeks ambiguity, subjectivity, and drift. Instead of modeling form, I am modeling feeling. This interface is not a precision instrument but a mood tuner.

I situate myself as a computational designer who believes maps are rhetorical objects — I am reimagining the routing algorithm as a playful, affective, and participatory medium. My audience includes designers, urbanists, and digital users frustrated with the flatness of conventional navigation tools. I want my tool to not be static, but dynamically changing based on the user. It is personalized and unique to each user.

Situated Technology
Informed by CDP’s emphasis on design as critique, this project explores how computational logic can be turned sideways: toward mood, ambiguity, and experiential pluralism. The dérive becomes not just an aesthetic reference, but a computational protocol — replacing cost functions with curiosity, direction with drift.

Aesthetic references: soft navigation (LoVid, Nold), playfully distorted GIS, semi-fictional overlays

Rhetorical Argument
This project argues that we must reclaim maps as tools of imagination.

If Google Maps tells us how to get there fastest, this tool asks: what if you don’t know where you’re going, only how you want to feel on the way?

The Serendipity Map is a rerouting of navigation toward joy, unpredictability, and personal drift — a computational dérive.

Brief Outline of a Potential Capstone Project
A working web-based routing interface built on NYC OpenStreetMap data.

Allows users to choose start/end and customize their walk by mood.

Generates real-time paths colored by serendipity and animated “mood zones”.

Potential for participatory layer: people annotate streets by emotion.

Output: live map, UI prototype, annotated diagrams, and narrative visualization

The Challenge
Technical: Incorporating subjective, fuzzy values into a routing algorithm.

Data: Finding or simulating fine-grained pedestrian/social data.

Conceptual: Translating psychogeographic theory into computational form without flattening it.

Visual: Balancing abstraction and readability in the output.

Personal Plan: Continue building Grasshopper logic, explore Mapbox interactivity, and collaborate with peers on data annotation layer. Data collection about cognitive distance vs real distance. Iterative prototyping of UI.
